"Sankranti and Makar Sankranti: Significance and Festivities"

```html
Sankranti Makar Sankranti
Sankranti refers to the transit of the Sun from one zodiac sign to another in the Hindu astrological calendar. This happens twelve times a year, once every month, and each transition has its own significance and associated festivals. The term 'Sankranti' is derived from 'Sat' (truth) and 'Kranti' (movement), indicating a change or a movement into a new sign.
Makar Sankranti is a specific Sankranti that occurs when the Sun transits into the zodiac sign of Capricorn (Makar in Sanskrit). It usually falls on January 14th and is celebrated as a major harvest festival across India. Makar Sankranti marks the end of the winter solstice and the start of longer days. It is associated with kite flying, feasts, and charitable activities. It is one of the few Hindu festivals that follow the solar cycle, unlike most festivals that are set by the lunar cycle of the lunisolar Hindu calendar.
Makar Sankranti is not the start of the Hindu New Year or calendar. The Hindu New Year varies regionally and is celebrated on different dates in different parts of India. For example, Ugadi marks the New Year in Andhra Pradesh and Karnataka, Gudi Padwa in Maharashtra, Puthandu in Tamil Nadu, and Navreh in Kashmir. These festivals typically fall in March or April, which is the month of Chaitra in the Hindu lunisolar calendar, marking the beginning of the new year.
```
